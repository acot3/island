Why attribute-based character creation?
Starting with D&D, pretty much every role-playing game (RPG) works the same way. You have background attributes that determine two things: accuracy and efficacy. Most of the time, this means chance of hitting and damage dealt. Final Fantasy is like this, Pokemon is like thisâ€¦ itâ€™s all the same model. Thereâ€™s also always a core randomization, which makes games fun by not letting them be predictable.D&D-Style ExampleA. Smith wants to break down the door. This requires STRENGTH. Smithâ€™s STRENGTH attribute is 15, giving them a +3 to STRENGTH rolls. Does Smith succeed? Breaking down a door is a hard thing to do, so it requires a roll of 15 or higher.
ROLL (13) + STRENGTH MODIFIER (3) = 16 >= DIFFICULTY (15); Smith succeeds at breaking down the door.
B. Jones wants to break down the door. This requires STRENGTH. Smithâ€™s STRENGTH attribute is 8, giving them a -1 to STRENGTH rolls. Does Jones succeed?
ROLL (13) + STRENGTH MODIFIER (-1) = 12 =< DIFFICULTY (15); Jones fails to break down the door.

Meta-Game
Certain highly successful games (e.g. Doki Doki Literature Club; Inscryption) have a meta aspect to them: the game is aware of itself as a game. For example, in Inscryption, you learn that you are playing a copy of a cursed floppy disc game that was buried in Stanley Park due to in-game reasons. The game makers actually buried a floppy in the park. There were hints in the game that led people to figure this out in an online community of sleuths, until a user actually dug it up. Pulling this off not only makes for a legendary game development achievement (Inscryption has a 5.0 on steam with 72k reviews, est. copies sold at 3M) but also immortalizes the game development company, making spin-offs hugely valuable.I donâ€™t quite see how it works yet, but I think that the AI aspect we have might be able to figure into a meta-game and into the multi-session nature of the product in an interesting way.
